Dividend Investors Have it All Wrong

A little understanding of dividends goes a long way.

“Dividends are free income.” If you’ve ever spoken to anyone who advocates dividend investing, I’m sure you’ve heard that phrase. If you know anything about me, you’ll know it’s one of the phrases I despise the most - and if you’re an investor, you probably should too.

It’s always clear to me which dividend investors understand how dividends actually work and which ‘dividend investors’ haven’t done their research. The former understand the power of cash flow while the latter know how easy it is to sell something to others with the promise of free money. The truth is dividend investing is the wrong investing approach for most people. I’m going to explain why - but first, let’s actually understand what a dividend is.

What is a dividend?#

Dividends are excess money a company has on their books that they give out to their shareholders. Be careful -  this is not a company paying you. This is money that is already reflected in your share price being disbursed into your account as cash. This is why the share price of a stock drops by the same amount paid out as a dividend. Therefore, your account reflects the same total value, but you have less money in the company and some money in cash. This isn’t a big deal, right? You can just reinvest in the same company and everything is the same.

Not quite.

Dividend disbursements are a taxable event. This means you pay tax on the cash that is now in your account. So while your account is valued the same after being paid a dividend, you now owe more in taxes. If that same stock hadn’t paid a dividend, your account would likely be the same value, just without owing taxes. Can you see the issue here? This isn’t free income. Over time, this tax burden can make the dividends cost you money.

This doesn’t mean dividends themselves are inherently bad. Just like any investment strategy, you need to know how they play into your stock market goals. The problem is once we know how dividends actually work, we begin to realize how incorrect the arguments for dividend investing actually are. Let’s go over a few and why they’re wrong:

“Dividends are free income.”#

With what we now know about dividends, this is inherently incorrect. This is the most common argument in favor of dividends, but has the worst consequences for (particularly new) investors. Only buying companies with dividend payouts make investors feel like they’re getting “free income”, when instead they’re paying more taxes.

Not only are these investors increasing their tax burden substantially, they’re also targeting companies that tend to have very little growth. This is a key misunderstanding for most dividend investors: growth is your income, the dividend is just the disbursement! If your shares are spitting out dividends but the share price isn’t rising, you just end up paying taxes on money you already own.

“The presence of a dividend is a way to identify a great company.”#

Similar to the relationship of rectangles and squares: there are many great companies that pay dividends, but not all companies that pay a dividend are great. The truth of the matter: the presence of a dividend tells us nothing about whether or not a company is a good investment. Even a dividend that increases throughout the years tells us nothing. To know if a company is a great investment we need a more complete picture of their operations and we do this by reading financial statements. Whenever you’re picking individual stocks this is a must.

Lazy investors try to bypass this step by just checking if a company pays a dividend. This only shows us that a company has extra cash on their books but more than just extra cash is needed for a company to be a great investment. If anything, the presence of a dividend should dissuade investors from investing in a company because there more investor-friendly ways a company can be using their extra cash - but that’s a topic for another time.

“Dividend stocks are safe, stable companies with slow steady growth.”#

If you’re investing for slow, steady growth there is a much better way to do it than investing in individual companies. Investing in an individual company is a calculated, riskier investment with the hope of that particular stock growing tenfold. If you want slow, steady, easy investing then you need a different investment vehicle.

This is where funds come in. Funds diversify your money between multiple different stocks to protect against the risk of any single stock doing poorly. And the best part? This investment is worry-free and takes very little time (and can even be automated!). No need to read financial statements. If your sole aim is for a safe investment, funds are a better bet than dividend stocks every time. Less time, less research, less worry, and quite often a better return.

So what are dividends good for then?#

Two words: cash flow. Dividends disburse money from your portfolio and they’re really good at it. While this will increase you tax burden long-term, this can be good if your goal is to disburse money into your account in the short-term. This can be great a strategy if you want to increase money available in your monthly budget or diversify your portfolio by investing dividend disbursements into other companies.

Takeaways#

There was a lot covered here and a good amount of it is nuanced. Here are some key takeaways:

  1. Dividends are not income. Growth is income. Dividends prioritize cash flow for the short-term and will hurt your portfolio growth in the long-term due to the additional tax burden. If your goal is long-term growth, your investment focus should not be dividends.

  2. Dividends are not a good metric for evaluating a stock. If you want to know whether a company is good investment you need to read financial statements. If you don’t want to read financial statements but still want growth, you’re better off investing in funds.

  3. Dividends are excellent for cash flow. If you want consistent cash flow to supplement your budget or to cash flow into other investments, dividends are an excellent way to do this. Just make sure you research your investment instead of only deciding by the dividend.

Hopefully this clears up how dividends actually work and why dividend investing is much less appealing than many make it out to be.
